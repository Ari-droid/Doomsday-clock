Origins of the Doomsday Clock

The Doomsday Clock is a symbol that represents the likelihood of a man-made global catastrophe, in the opinion of the members of the Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists. Maintained since 1947, the clock is a metaphor for threats to humanity from unchecked scientific and technological advances. The clock was originally created as a response to geopolitical insecurities and rising fear of nuclear war that could posibly lead to MAD(Mutually Assured Destruction). The clocks proximity to midnight determines the likelihood of impending doomsday and global catastrophe. Initially, the clock was set to 11:53 pm in 1947, but in 1953, with the testing of thermonuclear devices, the clock reached 11:58 pm, the most ominous of its readings since inception. By 1968 the clock moved back to 1948 due to the signing of the Strategic Arms Limitation Treaty(SALT 1) by the US and Soviet union. However, the countries then had a spike in tensions around 1984 with a failure to certify a second SALT agreement the clock moved to 11:57 pm. It remained there for a portion of the mid to late 1980s. In 1991 the clock moved all the way to 11:43 pm, the furthest from midnight it has ever been. This was a result of successful SALT II accords in 1991 by the US and Soviet Union. In 2007, the Science and security board decided that the clock should represent the risk of climate change and life science technological issues. In 2015, the clock moved back to 11:57 pm as a result of unchecked climate change, global nuclear weapons modernizations, and immense nuclear weapons arsenals. The clock then moved to 2 and a half minutes until midnight in 2017 landing at around 11:57:30s pm. Then in 2018 it reached 11:58 pm and remained there until now where it is currently at 11:58:20s pm.
